A Two-Speed Middle East: Regional Economic Transformation and Stagnation

In the Middle East, there is a stark divide between the economies of affluent Gulf states and the struggling economies of several other regional countries, which are facing a potential storm of high-inflation, unemployment, and broader macro-economic stagnation. As one recent study found, though the Gulf states represented only 15 percent of the region's total population, it had almost half of the total regional income. This tale of two development speeds is evident in the significant disparities among these countries in terms of GDP per capita and unemployment rates, and raises complex questions of how regional states should understand and respond to it.

Prosperity in the Gulf

With their vast oil and gas reserves and small populations (aside from Saudi Arabia), the Gulf states have a GDP per capita that rivals or surpasses that of many developed nations. The oil sector provided all Gulf countries with a strong starting point, and Bahrain, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Oman, and the United Arab Emirates have achieved significant advances in their living standards in just a few decades. Gulf sovereign wealth funds—valued at 3.7 trillion US dollars of assets by one analysis in early 2023—became tools for rapid diversification, providing access to advanced industries and tight integration with the global economy. More importantly, the expansion of other manufacturing and services sectors has helped Gulf states maintain growth despite slowdowns in the oil market.

As global energy dynamics shift and the world transitions towards renewable sources, the Gulf states are working to reimagine their economic structures to ensure sustained prosperity.

Saudi Arabia, the region's largest and fastest-growing G20 economy in 2022, has embarked on ambitious plans to transition away from oil dependence through its Vision 2030 initiative. By developing sectors such as tourism, technology, and entertainment, the Kingdom seeks to redefine its economic landscape. The UAE has been on a similar path for many years and is among the Gulf's most diversified economies. The UAE has become a global business and trade hub, attracting investment and talent from around the world; the country is also home to many multinational companies' regional headquarters.

States That Have Endured Conflict

On the other end of the spectrum lie countries that have been consumed by conflict, such as Libya, Syria, and Yemen. These states face an uphill battle in restoring security, rebuilding infrastructure, and creating an environment conducive to economic rejuvenation.

Libya, despite possessing significant natural resources (the country is ranked in the top ten globally for proven oil and gas reserves), has struggled to regain stability amid political fragmentation, persistent violent conflict, and the presence of competing armed groups. The country remains in a political stalemate with rival power centers claiming legitimacy for control of the country, and negotiations thus far having not produced an agreement on a constitutional framework that could pave the way toward stability and elections.

Ravaged by a decade-long civil war, Syria faces monumental challenges in rebuilding its shattered economy. The Arab League this year invited Syrian President Bashar al-Assad to attend for the first time since the country's suspension from the bloc in 2011 following the eruption of civil war. While diplomatic normalization is expected to facilitate some renewed growth and cross-border trade with Syria, further progress requires a comprehensive settlement on Syria's conflict that allows the return of Syrian refugees in Jordan, Lebanon, and elsewhere.

In Yemen, peace negotiations are progressing, but the country remains one of the world's largest humanitarian crises, with millions lacking access to safe water, sanitation and hygiene services, and regular outbreaks of vaccine-preventable diseases.

Challenges of Structural Reform

While the COVID-19 pandemic and factors like the war in Ukraine have caused significant economic shocks worldwide, some regional economic stagnation can be attributed to a combination of lack of natural resources, stagnant economies, and structural reform challenges.

The Egyptian economy has maintained positive growth rates despite difficulties such as rising food and energy prices after the war in Ukraine started early last year. However, it faces slowing growth, food insecurity, and declining foreign investment, which actors like the IMF have attributed to issues like excessive state control on macroeconomic factors like exchange rates and industrial sectors that most countries have privatized. According to the World Bank, Egypt must pursue reforms to unleash the private sector's potential by addressing barriers related to competition, skill shortages, and structural challenges related to its business environment, governance and the rule of law.

In Jordan, which has limited natural resources, there remains heavy reliance on external aid such as the more than 20 billion US dollars provided by the United States since 1951. The country continues to struggle with persistent unemployment challenges, including one of the region's lowest rates of women's participation in the labor force.

Lebanon, once seen as the Paris of the Middle East due to its vibrant culture and social life, is now a state grappling with the worst multi-pronged political and economic crisis in its history, with inflation reaching almost 270 percent this year. According to the World Bank, Lebanon should urgently embark on a comprehensive fiscal reform strategy and, in the medium term, must work to build better institutions, or else the social and economic situation could worsen.

Because of these factors, it will be difficult to facilitate development in some regional countries without addressing domestic considerations. International partners, including the IMF and World Bank, have called for major structural reforms to aid privatization, curtail public spending, and adjust monetary policy. Some governments like Jordan have cooperated with partners, and secured fiscal support for bailouts and structural adjustments. But domestic political obstructions remain in other cases. More generally, cuts to state public spending are likely to be socially destabilizing and place pressure on governments in their efforts to reform.

Discussion Questions

If the gap between regional economies widens, what role should international financial institutions such as the World Bank or the IMF play? What role should the international community play?

What domestic and regional conditions are necessary for states to use human and natural resources as a foundation for economic development, and what are the best ways to facilitate them?
